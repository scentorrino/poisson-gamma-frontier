**Recommendation:** Major Revisions
**Overall Score:** 66/100

## Summary
The paper proposes a Poisson stochastic frontier with Gamma-distributed inefficiency, deriving a marginal PMF (alternating series in production, one-dimensional integral in cost) and a conditional efficiency score, with a clean closed-form exponential ($\alpha=1$) corollary via the incomplete gamma function, a scaling model for inefficiency determinants, Monte Carlo validation, and an empirical application. The methodological contribution is genuine and well-positioned against Fé & Hofler (2013) and Greene (1990): replacing maximum simulated likelihood with deterministic (closed-form / quadrature) evaluation is a real, usable advance that count-data efficiency practitioners would value. However, the manuscript has a disqualifying internal inconsistency in its current state: the abstract, introduction, and conclusion advertise — in concrete detail — a patent/Hausman (1984) application that the paper does not deliver, while §6 estimates an entirely different EPA Clean Water Act non-compliance application. This must be fixed before the substantive contribution can be fairly judged, and it materially lowers my confidence in the manuscript's readiness.

## Major Comments

1. **The advertised application does not match the delivered application (most serious).** The abstract ("An empirical application to patent counts"), the introduction (§1, ¶7: "we apply the model to the @hausman1984 patent panel … and show that the exponential frontier improves over the baseline Poisson GLM, that capital intensity and science-sector membership are statistically significant determinants of inefficiency …"), and the conclusion (§7: "On the @hausman1984 patent panel the exponential frontier is precisely estimated and reproduces the textbook R&D elasticity, while the homogeneous Gamma fit cannot separately identify $\alpha$ and $b$") all describe a patent/R&D application with specific, named empirical findings. But §6 estimates an EPA NPDES Clean Water Act non-compliance application (cost orientation) with completely different covariates (NAICS sector, EPA region, inspections, percent minority), a different preferred model (Gamma + scaling, M5), and different findings (positive percent-minority effect, selection interpretation of enforcement). The §1 and §7 claims (capital intensity / science-sector determinants, "reproduces the textbook R&D elasticity," "homogeneous Gamma cannot separately identify $\alpha$ and $b$") appear nowhere in the delivered §6 and cannot be verified — they read as residue from an earlier draft. As a domain referee I cannot certify a contribution whose headline empirical claims are not the ones the paper actually produces.
   - **What would change my mind:** Make the front matter, §1, §6, and §7 describe one and the same application throughout. Either (a) commit to the EPA application and rewrite the abstract, the §1 contribution paragraph, and §7 to report the actual EPA findings (cost frontier, M5 preferred, the percent-minority / enforcement-selection results); or (b) restore the patent application in §6 and make §1/§7's specific claims verifiable. As-is, the mismatch alone warrants major revision regardless of the model's merits.

2. **Two empirical-application citations are missing from `references.bib`.** §6 (¶ on percent-minority, `tbl-cwa-Z-summary` discussion) cites `[@konisky2009; @grayShadbegian2004]` to support the environmental-justice claim that high-minority communities face systematically less monitoring scrutiny. Neither key exists in the bibliography (the bib contains 27 entries; these are not among them). This will fail to compile, and — more substantively — the *single most novel and policy-relevant empirical finding of §6* (positive $\hat\delta_{\text{minority}}$ interpreted as under-monitoring) rests entirely on these two uncited references. A domain referee will press hard on whether the environmental-justice interpretation is actually supported by the cited literature, and right now there is no cited literature.
   - **What would change my mind:** Add the two references with full bibliographic details, confirm they say what the paper claims (systematic under-monitoring in high-minority communities), and ideally add one or two more recent enforcement/EJ citations (e.g., on disparate NPDES enforcement) so the headline claim is not load-bearing on two sources.

3. **The EPA "feasible-minimum non-compliance frontier" framing needs an economic defense, not just a sign convention.** The cost-orientation framing treats observed non-compliance quarters as inflation above a "feasible minimum expected non-compliance count given facility characteristics," with inefficiency $u_i$ = "managerial inefficiency in compliance." This is a creative and defensible use of the cost frontier, but it is a substantive economic claim that referees in environmental/regulatory economics will scrutinize: (i) Is non-compliance genuinely an output a facility *minimizes*, or is some non-compliance strategic (penalties < abatement cost)? If the latter, the one-sided "inefficiency" conflates managerial slack with optimizing non-compliance — a different economic object. (ii) The frontier covariates include `log(1+inspections)`, which "loads positively … because larger or higher-risk facilities receive more inspections" — but inspections are plausibly endogenous to (and a consequence of) the very non-compliance being modeled, threatening the interpretation of $\lambda_i$ as an exogenous "feasible minimum." (iii) The interpretation of $\widehat{\mathrm{TE}}_i$ as "the proportion of the irreducible benchmark that facility $i$ achieves" is mechanically inherited from production SFA but its meaning for a *bad* output (violations) deserves explicit discussion.
   - **What would change my mind:** A paragraph distinguishing managerial-slack non-compliance from strategic/optimizing non-compliance and arguing which the model captures; a defense of inspection counts as a frontier covariate (or a robustness check excluding them / instrumenting); and an explicit statement of what TE = 0.6 means for a compliance bad.

4. **The contribution claim "to our knowledge the cost-frontier closed form has not previously been derived for count-data frontiers" (§2, after `eq-cost-pmf`) is a novelty assertion that needs more careful guarding.** This is a strong, citable claim. Fé & Hofler (2013) treat both orientations; Hofler & Scrogin (2008) is a count frontier; the negative-binomial-as-relaxed-constraint observation is folklore. The novelty here is specifically the *upper incomplete gamma closed form for the cost orientation under exponential inefficiency*. The paper should state precisely what is new (the closed form) vs. what is shared with prior work (the orientation framing), so a referee cannot dismiss the contribution as "Fé–Hofler with a different inefficiency distribution."
   - **What would change my mind:** Tighten the novelty statement to name the exact new object (cost-orientation incomplete-gamma likelihood and efficiency score), and add one sentence explicitly contrasting with Fé–Hofler's simulated-likelihood treatment of the cost case.

5. **Greene (1990) and the half-normal critique are leaned on heavily but the economic motivation for the *Gamma shape* in count settings is asserted more than demonstrated.** The interior-mode argument ("regulatory compliance costs, adjustment lags, or organisational slack") is plausible, but the paper's own simulations report that $\alpha$ is weakly identified (small persistent downward bias, delta-method under-coverage of $\hat\alpha$ and $\hat b$ that "does not attenuate with sample size"). A domain referee will ask: if $\alpha$ is hard to pin down and the exponential frontier "ranks units almost as accurately" (§7), what is the empirical payoff of the Gamma shape beyond an LR test rejecting $\alpha=1$? The paper needs to make the case that the shape parameter delivers economically different efficiency rankings or determinant conclusions, not merely a better likelihood.
   - **What would change my mind:** In §6, show that the preferred Gamma+scaling (M5) produces materially different efficiency rankings, determinant signs/magnitudes, or policy conclusions than the exponential (M4) — e.g., a rank-correlation or a table of how facility rankings shift. If they barely differ, say so and recommend the exponential as the practical default.

## Minor Comments

1. §1 still says "@sec-empirical presents the patent application" — inconsistent with the EPA §6 (same root cause as Major 1).
2. Abstract: "validated through Monte Carlo experiments covering both cases" — good, but the abstract never mentions the cost orientation or the scaling extension, both of which are now central. Consider advertising them.
3. §1 lists healthcare utilization, accidents, shipments as count-frontier applications but only one application is delivered; a sentence noting which orientation each example belongs to would strengthen the framing.
4. The intro's negative-binomial connection (relaxing $u_i \ge 0$ recovers NB) is elegant and underused as positioning — it could be elevated as a selling point ("the frontier is the NB with an incomplete-gamma likelihood").
5. §6 reports the mean compliance-efficiency score is low with "the bulk of facilities cluster near the lower end" — for a regulatory audience, contextualize whether a mean TE of (e.g.) ~0.5 is economically plausible given that 50%+ of facilities have zero violations (per `tbl-cwa-summary`'s zero share). A large zero mass with low mean efficiency needs reconciling.
6. `mutzBornmannDaniel2017` (Bayesian count SFA) is cited only in the conclusion as a future direction; it is relevant prior art and could be positioned in §1.
7. §6 dual-use of percent minority in both $x_i$ and $z_i$ is defended by appeal to the simulation; good, but consider a robustness check excluding it from the frontier.

## Missing Literature
- **Konisky (2009)** and **Gray & Shadbegian (2004)** — *cited in §6 but absent from `references.bib`.* Must be added; they are the sole support for the headline environmental-justice finding.
- **Recent disparate-enforcement / environmental-justice empirical work on NPDES or EPA enforcement** (beyond the two above) — a single-citation foundation for the paper's most policy-salient claim is thin for a field audience.
- **Strategic/optimizing non-compliance literature** (e.g., the regulatory-deterrence tradition) — needed to defend the "inefficiency = managerial slack" interpretation against the alternative that non-compliance is partly optimizing (Major 3).
- If the patent application is retained, **Pakes & Griliches (1980)** (already in the bib) and the surrounding patents–R&D literature should be engaged in §6, not just listed.

## Questions for the Authors
1. Which application is the paper's intended contribution — patents or EPA NPDES? The front matter and conclusion say one thing and §6 does another. Please reconcile and state the actual empirical findings consistently.
2. In the EPA application, is non-compliance an "inefficiency" (managerial slack) or partly a *choice* (when penalties are below abatement cost)? How does the model distinguish these, and what does $\widehat{\mathrm{TE}}_i$ mean for a facility that strategically tolerates violations?
3. Are inspection counts in the frontier $x_i$ exogenous to the non-compliance being modeled? If inspections respond to suspected non-compliance, does the "feasible minimum" interpretation survive?
4. Given the weak identification of $\alpha$ documented in your own simulations, what does the Gamma shape buy you *economically* in §6 relative to the exponential — different rankings, different determinant conclusions, or only a higher likelihood?
5. Do `konisky2009` and `grayShadbegian2004` actually document *under-monitoring* (lower inspection/enforcement intensity) in high-minority communities, as §6 claims? Please supply the references and confirm the mapping to a positive $\delta_{\text{minority}}$.
6. The conclusion claims the homogeneous Gamma "cannot separately identify $\alpha$ and $b$" on the patent panel — is this finding from the delivered EPA application, the (absent) patent application, or the simulations? Please ground every empirical claim in §7 in a result that actually appears in the paper.
